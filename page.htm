This is one of the best albums I own. This is one of the best albums I've ever heard. If I could go into my burning house to save just one album, I might grab this. And then cheat and also grab the first Modern Lovers LP, but I digress.

If I could put it another way: When this first came out, a local critic said he thought it would stand the test of time as at least the best album of the 1990s. I bought it for that reason, and I immediately agreed, and I even thought it would break open the whole alternative/underground scene. These days, every time I plunk the needle down on this, it still floors me that it in fact did not have the effect in 1990 that Nirvana's Nevermind did just a year later. It's that good, really.

Fugazi is no ordinary punk band. Fugazi is a punk band and much more. These guys are smart enough that they're not about to be content just playing three-chord runs at breakneck speed and scream over the noise, no, these guys know their guitars have six strings and they've figured out how to use them all, all up and down then neck, and in all kinds of sonically surprising ways.

They also know how to lock their instruments into tight grooves, as in the opener, "Turnover," a prime example of the kind of tight-loose push-pull thing these guys are so good at: Brendan Canty feathers his high-hat and brings bassist Joe Lally with him to groove along to the guitar work. It is that tight looseness that Fugazi may well have a patent on, and before you know it Ian MacKaye's guitar explodes and Guy Picciotto is rocking the mic.

And throughout the album, they rhyme, they sigh in tune, they lapse into French. And they can write amazing melodies and lyrics, and yes, even the Beatles reference is there. "I'm only sleeping," Picciotto insists. But where John Lennon started a revolution from his bed (in another firearm LP known as Revolver), Picciotto's character just wants to turnover and pretend that this is all a bad dream. And he notices similar laziness in his antagonist's tactics when he does confront him, "Lounge against your weapons until your muscles rot locked in the ease of that position."

No pause before the next song, and you're deep into noir territory, with things unfolding like the plot of the darkest detective story, only nobody's sure who the detectives are or who's done what to whom.

And, if you give it more than one listen, you'll find yourself coming back frequently, because this is punk with so much more to offer than angry youth slogans and punk rock guitar octaves. This is a rich and rewarding listening experience to last years.

Fugazi was founded by Ian Mackaye after the dissolution of hardcore punk pioneers Minor Threat. The sound of Repeater took what made Minor Threat great, the DIY ethos and raw energy and developed it, with some superb interplay between guitar and rhythm section and politically charged lyrics. Repeater melodically is bass driven, taking some cues from post-punk in this respect, with intricate bass hooks from Joe Lally defining the majority of the songs here. Indeed it's the rhythm section that kick the album off in "Turnover" with a bass arpeggio that forms the basis of the song. While the guitar takes over in this instance to provide the main riff, "Turnover" shows that Fugazi's brand of punk utilises all of the main rock instruments, and not just in a way that simply is a band playing as loudly and fast as possible.

Of course some of Repeater is going to be loud and fast. It's a punk album! Some of its best moments are when the band completely rocks out. "Merchandise" for example is one of the best straight forward punk songs of all time. Driven by the rhythm section once again with a brilliant bass hook in the verse, its a tirade against consumerism ("What could a businessman ever want more?/Than to have us sucking in his store!") before it comes to a huge loud chorus with raw guitars and MacKaye screaming over them "We owe you nothing! You have no control!". It's powerful enough for you to want to throw all your branded goods out of the window and go and vandalise the nearest Tesco. "Styrofoam" is another example, MacKaye's anger about racism over a soaring guitar riff. Sometimes they can slow down a little and still be great though, "Blueprint" has an incredibly simple but effective guitar hook, but works very well melodically in terms of both vocals and instrumentals.

While much of this record's lyrical content is political, there are times when the band go to their other main characteristic, the straight-edge lifestyle. Two highlights here are anti-drugs songs, "Sieve-Fisted Find" is fast and desperate, a song from the perspective of a drug addict again driven by some excellent bass work and an overbearing sense of urgency from Guy Picciotto's vocals (him and MacKaye alternate vocal duties). The other one is closer "Shut The Door", perhaps the slowest song on the album, but also the darkest. Creepy, tense and menacing throughout, it has great dynamics changes with a quiet verse about the darkness of a crack den and the destructiveness of drug addiction "I burn a fire to stay cool I burn myself, I am the fuel." before the loud chorus with MacKaye manically screaming about the final effects of overdose "She's not breathing!/She's not moving!/She's not coming back!". It's a disturbing closer and a contrast to the defiance of the preceding ten songs.

Repeater is everything that should be expected from a punk album. It's loud, fast, angry and rebellious but manages to be intelligent with it at the same time. It also manages to bring more to the table than the usual three chords with some stunning bass work taking the forefront. It's punk done well, and so much more.

Fugazi-Repeater *****

Repeater is easily the most important album of the 1990's. Forget Nevermind or anything else that comes to mind, Fugazi's repeater hands down takes the cake. While it was far from the commercial success of albums by Nirvana or Pearl Jam, or even that of Sonic Youth it had something all those albums didn't have. The talent is stronger in Fugazi for both musical ability and songwriting, but most importantly because the band had heart. Sonic Youth was at a time of despair in their career, Nirvana was great but Kurt said it himself he just does what he does and it means nothing, and Pearl Jam are to Fugazi what U2 were to The Clash....wannabes.

'Turnover' 'Merchandise' 'Sieve-Fisted Find' and 'Shut The Door' say more together than just about any other song from the generation. Ian's lyrics have always been something of a head turner but Repeater finds him more than any other album be it by Fugazi, Minor Threat, or his other project the Evens writing compelling and influential lyrics. Musicianship wise this is the guitar album of the 1990's. No debating it.

Repeater is Fugazi's most straight forward album and probably their strongest effort over all. The inclusion of 3 Songs ep doesn't hurt either. But why is it the most important album of the 1990's? Because it says what every other whiny band of the era wanted to say but without whining and with a solution offered.

Ribena! is how MacKaye's patented shouty hardcore delivery of the title track's chorus decodes in my brain. Had the succeeding lyric been something to the effect, ...is a fruit juice substitute stripped of nutritional value through mass production in capital's interests, but marketed like the opposite has been done in yours, then we'd be dealing with serious punk rock. Unfathomably, it turns out my brain is simply unreliable.

Instead, the song seems to prod at weightier ideas about personal atomisation and homogeneity engendered by the exchange of labour from Ian's experience as both worker and petit-bourgeois Dischord tyrant lol. Not something that lends itself readily to musical form though. Anticapitalism is Fugazi's bag, but it's not Minor Threat's primal, teenage, absolute rejection, which seems more immediately intelligible precisely for its lack of an analysis: kids renounce cultural norms and found their own because they can neither describe nor overthrow their prescribed roles; easy. Fugazi Minor Threat + an analysis - vivid punk subject matter such as how Ribena is in fact SATAN'S DIABETIC URINE. Instead, MacKaye's efforts to encapsulate nebulous ideas about people and political economy produce no less nebulous results. I have no interest in deciphering what a 'Sieve-fisted Find' (ugh) is when they could be distilling capital's parasitic nature in angry songs about Ribena. They really needed to do that Ribena idea.

Repeater's best track ('Ribena' a close second) is 'Blueprint', whose moody chords were majorly ripped off by The Get Up Kids and made into Eudora's 'The Breathing Method', judged to be a superior song by those unafraid to admit they like The Get Up Kids' artless melodrama. Indeed, 13 Songs and even Red Medicine once it's stopped trying to kid you into thinking they'd hired Steve Albini to play atonal guitar honk on it are better depictions of the hardcore sound's aftermath.
